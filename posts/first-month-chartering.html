First month in chartering — what stood out to me early on

A short reflection on the first month at German Tanker Shipping: what felt different from my expectations, what I noticed early, and why the work looks different once you are actually inside it.

One month is obviously not enough to understand chartering properly, but it is enough to realise that the work feels very different once you are actually sitting inside it.

Before I started, I mostly thought about chartering through the usual words: freight, fixtures, market logic, negotiations. Those things are still there, but being in the office makes the day-to-day side much more visible. You start noticing how much of the work depends on timing, how carefully information has to be handled, and how quickly small details can matter when they sit inside a live commercial process.

One of the first things that stood out to me was how much attention the work needs. Not in a dramatic way, just in a disciplined way. You pay attention to how people communicate, how information moves, how records are kept, and how the desk stays organised enough for decisions to actually be made. From the outside, that part is easy to underestimate.

The month also made the relationship between theory and practice much clearer to me. At university, you learn concepts, terms, and structure. At work, those things stop feeling abstract because they attach themselves to real tasks and real conversations. That has probably been the most useful part so far.

Another thing I noticed quite early is that the commercial side of shipping feels much more human than it can look from a distance. Of course numbers matter, but so do coordination, trust, timing, and the way people work together. That part became much clearer once I was inside the environment instead of just thinking about it from outside.

After the first month, I do not feel like I know a lot. If anything, I see more clearly how much there is still to learn. But I do feel more certain that this is the side of shipping I want to stay close to, because the work feels real, demanding, and worth taking seriously.

That is really all this reflection is meant to say. Not something big, just an honest record of what the first month changed for me and how it made the work feel more concrete than it did before.
