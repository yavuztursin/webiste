Focus Areas

What I read and track.

A deliberately narrow set of themes: the commercial mechanics of tanker shipping and the market forces behind them.

  1. 01 Product tanker freight MR and LR segments. How refinery runs and arbitrage economics show up in rates.
  2. 02 Crude tanker cycles VLCC, Suezmax, Aframax. Why rate cycles form the way they do.
  3. 03 Tonne-mile dynamics Red Sea, Panama, and other route disruptions. How rerouted trade reshapes fleet utilisation.
  4. 04 Shipping politics and sanctions How regulation, sanctions, and political decisions reshape trade routes, fleet use, and freight demand.
  5. 05 Refinery geography and arbitrage Where margin forces cargoes to move, and how that shows up on the freight side.
  6. 06 Fleet supply Orderbook, scrapping, yard capacity, and age profile. The tonnage side of the cycle.
  7. 07 Voyage economics and charter-party logic TCEs, bunkers, canal and port costs, and the arithmetic behind each fixture.

Professional Direction

How I am approaching the work.

  1. Depth before breadth.

    Shipping rewards real understanding more than fast résumé building. I would rather get depth in tanker markets than move too quickly and stay superficial.

  2. Connect desk work to market structure.

    Rates, utilisation, and fixture decisions are not isolated desk numbers. They come from trade flows, refinery geography, fleet supply, weather, and disruptions. The work is learning to connect those layers properly.

  3. Use writing as a working tool.

    Writing is useful because unclear thinking becomes obvious on the page. The notes section is there to make ideas more precise, not to fill space.

  4. Stay close to live commercial work.

    The fastest learning in shipping happens where cargoes, tonnage, and decisions are live. That is the environment I want to stay close to while building experience.
